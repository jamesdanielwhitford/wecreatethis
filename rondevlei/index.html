Species at a Glance

Protea repens (Suikerbos)

Most forgiving. Roots in 8–10 weeks. Tolerates slightly heavier soils. Flowers May–Oct, peak Jun–Aug. Good first species.

Protea cynaroides (King)

Slowest (12–16 weeks rooting, 12+ months in bags). Cut Feb only. Highest cut-flower value. Frost tolerant once established. Flowers Nov–Mar.

Leucadendron

Easiest. Roots 6–10 weeks. Take cuttings Dec–Mar. Male and female separate — cut from known females for foliage. Flowers Apr–Sep.

Leucospermum (Pincushion)

Cuttings Mar–Apr after flowering growth hardens. Needs bottom heat. Sensitive to overwatering. Flowers Aug–Nov, peak Sep–Oct.

Erica species

Most difficult. Small cuttings (5–8cm). April only. Pure sand medium. High failure rate early on. Field plant April not March.

Restios (Cape Reed)

Not from cuttings — divide clumps or grow from seed in March–April. Fastest establisher. Best groundcover and mulch source for the Fukuoka system.

Phase 1
Taking Cuttings
Primary: February–March  |  Secondary: August–September

Proteas are propagated from semi-hardwood cuttings — the current season's new growth that has firmed up over several months but is not yet old woody stem. Getting the maturity right is the single most important factor.

Feb–Mar at Sedgefield: avg high 23.5–24.5°C, avg low 16–17°C, RH 94%. Warm and humid — good for parent plant vigour and cutting survival. Take cuttings early morning when humidity is highest.

Aug–Sep: avg high 17.9–18.5°C, avg low 8–9.9°C, RH 88–91%. Cooler and drier. Cuttings root more slowly but are less prone to fungal rot.

Selecting the right shoot

1

Look for shoots from the current season's growth that pushed out in spring (Sep–Nov) and has spent 3–5 months firming up. By Feb–Mar these are the right maturity.

2

Bend test: stem should bend fairly easily but not flop. It should not bend back on itself. Snaps cleanly and is slightly springy — that is semi-hardwood. Floppy tips fail. Old woody stems fail.

3

Best cuttings come from side shoots just below a forming bud or old flower head — highest natural rooting hormone concentration.

4

Cut 10–15cm long, cleanly below a node with sharp sterilised secateurs. Take cuttings early morning — never in afternoon heat.

5

Place cut ends immediately into clean water. Process within a few hours. If storing overnight, wrap in damp cloth and refrigerate.

Preparing the cutting

1

Strip leaves from the bottom two-thirds of stem. Leave 4–6 leaves at tip. In Feb heat, reduce to 3–4. In Aug, 5–6 is fine.

2

Wound the base: two shallow vertical cuts opposite each other in the bark at the bottom 2cm. Exposes cambium and speeds rooting. On thin stems, light sanding works.

3

Dip bottom 2cm into IBA rooting hormone at 4000 ppm for 5 seconds (liquid dip), or gel/powder rated for semi-hardwood. Do not skip — proteas are slow rooters and hormone treatment significantly improves success.

Do not take cuttings from stressed, flowering, or drought-stressed plants. Fertilise mother plants with low-phosphorus feed 6 weeks before cutting to improve shoot vigour.
Phase 2
Rooting in the Propagation Bed
Duration: 8–16 weeks from cutting to rooted plant

The cutting has no roots and cannot replace water lost through its leaves. Keep humidity high enough that transpiration stays low, while keeping the medium airy enough to prevent fungal rot.

Sedgefield advantage: morning RH of 88–95% year-round means you do not need an elaborate enclosed propagator. The main risk is warm afternoons (22–24°C in Feb–Mar) drying cuttings fast if exposed.

Rooting medium

Mix recipe

  • 2 parts coarse washed river sand (not beach sand — salt kills proteas)
  • 1 part peat moss or well-composted pine bark
  • Optional: 10% perlite to improve drainage further

Target pH 5.0–5.5. No compost, fertiliser, or nutrient-rich material — protea roots are mycorrhizal and rich medium suppresses root development.

Container

  • Small clear plastic bags (500ml–1L) with air holes punched in sides, or 6–8cm pots
  • Clear bags let you see roots forming without disturbing the cutting
  • Fill with moist (not wet) rooting mix — clumps when squeezed but does not drip
  • Insert cutting to one-third of its length. Firm gently around base.

Shade tunnel setup

  • Simple tunnel frame — PVC pipe or bent rebar, 80–120cm tall
  • 50% shade cloth — essential in Feb–Mar. 30% adequate in winter
  • Orient opening south or southeast to avoid direct afternoon sun
  • Optional: clear plastic over shade cloth at night to trap humidity, open in morning for airflow
  • Bottom heat mat set to 22–24°C — root temp matters more than air temp. Warm roots, cooler leaves is optimal

Watering

  • Mist leaves twice daily in summer (morning and late afternoon), once daily in winter
  • Check medium moisture every 3–4 days — barely damp is correct. Bottom-water by sitting pots in shallow tray for 10 minutes if dry
  • Never let cuttings sit in standing water
  • Use rainwater if possible — tap water may have chlorine and high pH. Let tap water stand 24hrs in open container first

Phase 3
Potting On into Nursery Bags
When roots visible on both sides — typically month 3 to 4

Once roots are well developed the cutting becomes a plant and needs a proper growing bag — still nutrient-poor, still very free-draining.

Bag setup

  • Black plastic nursery bags, 2–3 litre (roughly 15cm wide, 20cm tall). Black prevents algae on roots and insulates against temperature swings
  • 6–8 drainage holes in bottom and lower sides
  • Raise bags off ground on slatted bench or bricks for drainage and airflow

Grow-on medium (different from rooting mix)

  • 2 parts coarse river sand
  • 2 parts composted pine bark (well composted, pH neutral — not fresh)
  • 1 part perlite

Still no fertiliser. Proteas form proteoid cluster roots that extract phosphorus efficiently from poor soil. Fertiliser destroys these roots. Wait until 15cm+ and actively growing, then only dilute seaweed or low-phosphorus liquid feed.

Potting on process

1

Water the rooted cutting the day before so the root ball holds together.

2

Fill bag one-third with grow-on mix. Tear rooting bag away carefully. Place root ball, backfill gently. Stem base at same depth as before — do not bury deeper.

3

Water in gently. Place in 50% shade for 2 weeks while plant adjusts, then move to morning sun only position.

Growing on

Target: 25–35cm height before field planting. Takes 4–8 months at Sedgefield.

Watering: when top 2–3cm feels dry. Summer: every 2 days. Winter: every 4–6 days. Always morning. Never afternoon in summer — wet bags in hot sun causes phytophthora root rot.
  • Keep under 30–40% shade cloth — cut harshest midday sun but still bright
  • Space bags so leaves do not touch — airflow prevents disease
  • Pinch growing tip once at 15cm to encourage branching
  • Watch for: few yellow older leaves (normal), complete yellowing (overwatering or wrong pH), black stem at base (phytophthora — remove immediately)
Phase 4
Hardening Off
3–4 weeks before field planting — start late February for March planting

Plants grown under shade cloth are not ready for direct field conditions. Harden off gradually over 3–4 weeks.

1

Week 1: Move from 40% to 20% shade. Water normally. If leaves droop at noon, move back to more shade for a few more days.

2

Week 2: Full morning sun, shade cloth for afternoon only. Reduce watering frequency by 20%.

3

Weeks 3–4: Remove shade cloth entirely. Water only when bags feel dry at 3cm depth. Plants should not wilt even in afternoon sun.

Field-ready signs: firm stems in moderate wind, dark green leaves, visible root structure at drainage holes, 25–35cm height, no stress or disease.
Phase 5
Field Planting
Primary: March–April  |  Secondary: September (wet years only)
Why March–April: ET0 drops from 4.6mm/day in Jan to 2.5mm/day in April. Temps cool from 24°C to 21°C. Rain is reliable — March averages 48mm, April 51mm across 8 years with no year below 24mm in March. The plant gets 5–6 months of cool, moist establishment before its first summer stress.

Site preparation (January–February)

  • Minimal preparation is correct for sand fynbos — do not add compost or topsoil
  • Clear alien vegetation in 60cm radius per plant. Leave native fynbos groundcover in place
  • If heavily compacted, loosen a 40cm diameter, 30cm deep hole with a fork only — do not fill with amendments
  • Lay 10–15cm deep mulch of dry fynbos material (restio straw, leucadendron prunings, leaf litter) in 50cm radius around each planting spot

Planting day

1

Choose a day after rain — soil moist 5cm down. Never plant into bone-dry soil.

2

Soak nursery bag in water 10–15 minutes immediately before planting. Root ball should be thoroughly wet.

3

Dig a hole the same depth as the root ball, 20% wider. No deeper — planting too deep causes collar rot.

4

Remove bag keeping root ball intact. Gently tease any circling roots outward — do not break them.

5

Place plant in hole. Backfill with the same sand — no amendments. Firm gently. Stem base (soil level in bag) sits exactly at ground level — not below.

6

Water in deeply with 5–10 litres. Mulch around plant (not touching stem) to 10cm depth.

Phase 6
Harvesting Cut Flowers
Year 2–3 onwards. Peak quality: June–August.

Proteas are among the best cut flowers in the world — long vase life, dramatic form, and they dry beautifully. Getting the harvest right comes down to picking at the correct bract stage, cutting cleanly, and conditioning immediately.

Sedgefield sits between the winter-rainfall Western Cape and summer-rainfall Eastern Cape, giving an extended flowering window across species.

When to cut — the bract stage

1

Too early (hard-tip): bracts tightly closed, firm, pointed. Flowers cut here will not open. Do not cut.

2

Correct (soft-tip): outer bracts have begun to loosen and separate slightly but still cohere as a unit. Bract tips have softened. Inner florets not yet visible. A few insects beginning to investigate — a good field indicator. Cut now.

3

Too late (open): bracts fully splayed, inner florets visible and shedding pollen. Vase life is halved. Still usable but will not last.

How to cut

1

Cut in early morning — stems fully turgid, temps lowest. Never cut in afternoon at Sedgefield's summer highs of 24°C+.

2

Sharp, clean secateurs or knife. Blunt cuts crush vascular tissue and reduce water uptake. Sterilise with methylated spirits or dilute bleach between plants.

3

Cut at 45-degree angle, leaving at least 2–3 nodes below the cut on the plant. This allows re-shooting and next season's flowers. Never cut into old grey wood.

4

40–60cm stem length minimum for commercial use. Short-stemmed proteas lose most of their market value.

5

Place stems immediately into clean water in shade. Even a few minutes exposed to sun or wind desiccates the cut end severely.

6

Strip all leaves from the bottom 20–25cm of stem — submerged leaves rot fast, foul water, and kill the flower within days.

Post-harvest conditioning

Do this within 1–2 hours of cutting. This step determines 1 week versus 3 weeks of vase life.

1

Re-cut under water: submerge bottom 5cm in a bucket, make a fresh 45-degree cut. Prevents air entering vascular tissue.

2

Stand in clean water with 1 tablespoon bleach per litre. Controls bacteria that block vascular tissue. Or use commercial flower food at correct dilution.

3

Place in coolest available space for minimum 4 hours, ideally overnight. Target 8–12°C. In Jun–Aug at Sedgefield, a cool shed or garage is naturally perfect.

4

Storage beyond 2–3 days: refrigerate at 4–6°C. Do not store near ethylene-producing fruit (bananas, apples, avocados) — ethylene accelerates ageing rapidly.

Leaf blackening

The main post-harvest problem. Caused by oxidation of phenolic compounds, accelerated by warmth, water stress, and ethylene.

  • Harvest at soft-tip — over-mature flowers blacken faster
  • Condition immediately — any water stress triggers blackening
  • Keep below 10°C — blackening slows dramatically in cool temps
  • Remove blackened leaves as they appear — they accelerate neighbouring leaf blackening
  • Do not mist leaves after harvest — wet leaves in warm conditions blacken fast
  • If no refrigeration: harvest in Jun–Aug when Sedgefield's ambient temps naturally slow the process

Key Climate Facts for Propagation

Humidity advantage

Morning RH averages 88–95% year-round. You do not need an elaborate misting system. Orient your shade tunnel to capture morning humidity and you have a natural propagation environment.

Frost risk is rising

Nights below 3°C: zero in 2018–2020, now occurring most winters. Record low 1.0°C in July 2023. Build frost cloth capability before June — simple shade cloth tunnel with plastic overnight cover is enough.

Dry spells lengthening

Longest annual dry spell has grown from 12–16 days (2018–2023) to 18 days (2024) and 22 days (2025). Deep mulch (10–15cm) cuts surface evaporation 60–70% and is the single most effective response.
